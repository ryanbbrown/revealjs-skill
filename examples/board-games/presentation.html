Complexity vs. Depth in Board Games

How rules, decisions, and mastery shape player experience

Based on analysis from BoardGameGeek community data

Why This Talk

  • Understand where "BGG weight" fits—and doesn't
  • Walk through a 0–10 ladder using well-known games as signposts
  • Leave with heuristics to evaluate games for your audience

Design Lenses

  • MDA Framework: Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics
  • Classic mantra: great games deliver "a series of interesting decisions"

Why Complexity and Depth Diverge

  • High depth, low rules: Chess/Go—small rule sets, enormous mastery curves
  • High rules, modest depth: Campaign crawlers with heavy administration but limited skill separation
  • Implication: Don't equate "hard to learn" with "worth mastering"

Estimating Complexity

Practical checklist:

  • Teach time
  • Rulebook length
  • Exceptions density
  • Setup/admin time
  • Iconography load
  • Hidden info/simultaneous steps
  • Number of "moving parts"
BGG "weight" as a signal, not truth—it's crowdsourced and blends factors.

Estimating Depth

Practical checklist:

  • Is there meaningful choice each turn?
  • Skill separation: do experts consistently beat novices?
  • Meta diversity: multiple viable strategies?
  • Learning curve: new layers after 5/20/100 plays?
Sirlin's test: "still strategically interesting after years by experts."

The 0–10 Ladder

From no choices to lifelong strategic sandboxes

Level 0 — "Shape Sorters"

Thesis: No choices → no complexity or depth

Example: Candy Land (task following)

Watch for: Use only as a teaching tool for turn order, win/loss, reading a board

Level 1 — "Games for the Elderly"

Thesis: Ultra-light rules; shallow optimization under heavy randomness

Example: Camel Up — teach in minutes; fun probabilistic reads

BGG Weight: ≈ 1.50

Depth capped by dice/leg randomness.

Level 2 — "I'm Uncomfy When I Don't Know What To Do"

Thesis: Simple loop, novel theme; hidden teams add perceived complexity

Example: Secret Hitler — voting & agenda deck create drama

BGG Weight: ≈ 1.74

Rubber-band systems and swinginess constrain mastery.

Level 3 — "The Cardboard Wall"

Thesis: The classic entry tier that converts people into hobbyists

Example: Catan — trades, negotiation, modular maps

BGG Weight: ≈ 2.29

Strategy exists but ceiling constrained by setup & dice.

Level 4 — "What do you mean I have to read?"

Thesis: Reading comprehension & inference become necessary

Example: Blood on the Clocktower — high logical & bluffing depth

BGG Weight: ≈ 3.03

Storyteller adjudication adds exception load.

Level 5 — "Achy Breaky Brain"

Thesis: Fewer actions, lots of nuance; depth drives difficulty

Example: Brass: Birmingham — six actions, network & market timing

BGG Weight: ≈ 3.87

Huge decision space; evolving metas.

Level 6 — "Dude, where's my Kallax?"

Thesis: Interlocking systems; moderate rules load, high situational judgment

Example: Dune: Imperium – Uprising — leaders, spies, multi-currency tradeoffs

BGG Weight: ≈ 3.51

Healthy competitive meta; some strong openings.

Level 7 — "The Sane Person's Limit"

Thesis: Big cognitive load—especially asymmetric factions

Example: Root (+expansions) — 10+ factions with unique economies

BGG Weight: ≈ 3.83

Mastery needs understanding your and others' engines. Diplomacy/king-slaying dynamics affect outcomes.

Level 8 — "Nutty Putty"

Thesis: Campaign scale introduces persistence/admin

Example: Gloomhaven — heavy admin; tactical hand management

BGG Weight: ≈ 3.91

Deep content, moderate skill depth. Scenario setup & AI upkeep dominate complexity.

Level 9 — "996"

Thesis: Lifestyle games: encyclopedic content + politics

Example: Twilight Imperium 4e + Prophecy of Kings — 25 factions, negotiation, agendas

BGG Weight: base 4.34; PoK 4.41

Deep tactical & political play, kingmaking pressures late.

Level 10 — "Complexity without pain"

Thesis: Maximal asymmetry + exceptions + multiple rule traditions

Example: Dune (1979 lineage, GF9 2019 edition) — deterministic combat + alliance/politics

BGG Weight: ≈ 3.99

Once digested, lifelong strategic sandbox. Competing rule sets increase onboarding complexity.

Case Studies

Examining the complexity vs. depth relationship in practice

Case Study A: Depth > Complexity

Brass: Birmingham

  • Why it feels hard: interdependent decisions (network, markets, tempo)
  • Signals of depth: multiple viable arcs, sharp skill separation, evolving metas, low randomness
  • Teaching tip: front-load network/connectedness concepts

Case Study B: Complexity > Depth

Gloomhaven

  • Why it feels hard: setup/admin, scenario rules, AI upkeep
  • Where depth levels off: bounded hand-management puzzles; randomness spikes
  • Teaching tip: organization systems/inserts reduce perceived complexity

Case Study C: Asymmetry & Diplomacy

Root vs. TI4

  • Shared pattern: strong table-talk and king-slaying incentives
  • Depth levers: knowledge of all factions, tempo of scoring, diplomacy credibility
  • Teaching tip: establish norms (e.g., support-for-the-throne in TI4)

Where Does BGG Weight Help—and Mislead?

Helpful:

  • Quick proxy for teach burden
  • Rough within-genre comparisons

Misleading:

  • Blends complexity & depth
  • Subject to audience bias
  • Prefer separate lenses when evaluating

Research Corner

For the curious:

  • MDA framework — rules → play patterns → fun
  • Sirlin — formal definition of depth, balance
  • Sid Meier — "interesting decisions" as design yardstick
  • GameDeveloper — comprehension vs. strategic depth

Heuristics You Can Apply Tomorrow

  • Two-minute complexity scan: exceptions density, setup minutes, reference card count
  • Depth probes: list 3 viable strategies; ask "what beats what?"
  • Teach plan: swap 10 minutes of rules for guided play
  • Group fit: align complexity with group bandwidth

Takeaways

  • Complexity is the cost to play
  • Depth is the value to keep playing
  • Use BGG weight cautiously; validate with checklists
  • Choose/teach/design for audience bandwidth, then grow depth over time
